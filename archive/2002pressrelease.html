PRESS RELEASE

10 September 2002

THE INTERNATIONAL COASTAL CLEAN-UP SINGAPORE 2002

 

The Raffles Museum of Biodiversity Research (RMBR) at the National University of Singapore will spearhead and coordinate the clean-up of the mangrove section of the International Coastal Clean-Up Singapore (ICCS) 2002 on Saturday 14 September 2002 from 8.30 am to 4.00 pm at Kranji mangroves. Low tide clean-up operations will be from 8.30 am to 11.00 am while high tide removal of accumulated trash bags will take place from 11am to 4.00 pm.

 

Similarly, more than 1,500 volunteers will take to the beaches in another clean-up operation which is co-organised by the Nature Society (Singapore) the following Saturday 21 September 2002. This clean-up event, which will take place from 8.00 am to 12 noon at the various beaches in Singapore, will involve numerous schools and organizations.

 

The objectives of the International Coastal Clean-up are to:

  • Remove debris from the shorelines, waterways, and beaches of the world's lakes, rivers, and oceans;
  • Collect valuable information on the amount and types of debris
  • Educate people on the issue of marine debris, and
  • Use the information collected from the Clean-up to effect positive change on all levels, from the individual to the international to reduce marine debris and enhance marine conservation.

 

The International Coastal Cleanup is the largest marine pollution cleanup effort currently in existence. The event heightens public awareness about the vast problem of marine debris, and more importantly unites citizens from many nations in an attempt to do something about pollution in their communities. The ultimate goal is to eliminate the need for such clean-ups by deterring people and industries from polluting our waters in the first place.

 

Now in its 11th year in Singapore, the International Coastal Clean-up 2002 is part of an international effort involving over 700,000 volunteers from 77 countries, coordinated by the Ocean Conservancy in the US.

 

This year, more than 400 participants from St. Andrew's Junior College's Green Club, Singapore American School's SAVE Club and Middle School, SIF-ASEAN Student Fellowship, Temasek Junior College's Green Instinx, Woodlands Ring Secondary, Yusoff Ishak Secondary School, Beatty Secondary School, Commonwealth Secondary School, and NUS' Friends of the Raffles Museum, Medical Society and Faculty of Environmental Engineering will take part in the event. They will be supported by Tao Nan School's Marlin Sea Scouts and members of the Republic of Singapore Yacht Club during the high tide operations. Altvater Jakob Pte Ltd will assist in the disposal of the trash.

 

The data accumulated at the end of this clean-up, like those from all over the world, is collated and submitted to the global coordinator, the Ocean Conservancy. The data is analysed and used to raise legislative issues at the United Nations and at national level, to promote public education programmes and provide feedback to the government.

 

This is the 6th mangrove clean-up since a mangrove section was initiated in Singapore in 1997. The chosen site is the Kranji mangroves, north-west of Kranji Reservoir, off the western Straits of Johore. It tackles a historical accumulation of marine trash located in the inner reaches of the forest. Last year, after just 90 minutes, participants removed nearly three tonnes of trash from the Kranji mangroves, of which 90% was plastic debris. The mangrove section of the International Coast Cleanup Singapore is supported by a Ford Motor Company Conservation and Environmental grant.
